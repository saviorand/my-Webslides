Immigration and Inequality

David Card, UC Berkeley
Excerpt by Valentin Erokhin

Goal:.

Review and synthesize current views on relationship between immigration and inequality

Approach:

Use evidence from cross-city comparison of U.S for that

Cross-city comparison

  • → Others say it's inherently flawed

    but evidence is consistent with time series data
  • Problem with cross-city comparisons: immigrants and natives move easily between cities and if the flows are sensitive to local wages this can disrupt the picture

  • Aggregate time series

    Are proposed to fix this problem Consistent with cross-city if parameters are appropriately identified

Key Findings

  • Workers with pre-high-school diploma are perfect substitutes for w/ high school diploma
  • "High school-equivalent" and "College-equivalent" workers are imperfect substitutes
  • Natives and immigrants are imperfect substitutes

When demand side is parametrized w/ these assumptions and capital is assumed to be perfectly elastically supplied the impact of net inflows of immigrants on subgroups of native workers is quite small

Cross-city comparison

  • Elasticity of substitution immigrant vs. native is large but finite

  • → They are imperfect substitutes

  • → Impact of additional immigrant inflows concentrated in immigrant populations!

  • Empirically residual wage inequality is much higher for natives in high-immigrant cities
  • But comparisons by sub period and within narrow occupations rule out significant causal role

But there is an effect on overall wage inequality

about 5% between 1980 and 2000
that's because there is more immigrants on the tails of distribution
& residual inequality is higher among immigrants

Conceptual (theoretical) framework

Assumptions

  • Perfectly inelastic per capita labor supply (for local markets number of workers can vary)
  • Capital separable from labor inputs and perfectly elastically supplied
