De-industrialization and immigrants?

by Valentin Erokhin

Goal:.

Check how de-industrialization level affects distributional outcomes of immigrants and natives

Approach:

Use several inequality indices and oaxaca-blinder decomposition, as well as public de-industrialization metrics

De-industrialization levels

  • United Kingdom

    - Manufacturing share of employment fell from a third in 1970s to about 10 percent today, real MVA at 2005 prices declined from a quarter to less than 15 percent

  • Germany

    - About 2-3 times larger manufacturing sector than the UK
    - Manufacturing is about 20% of all employment
    - Still slowly deindustrializes (IZA says decline is entirely due to young entrants and non-employment returnees + process cannot be attributed to globalization)

More migrants in the UK?

  • Poor man's approach to defining migrants: People whose country of birth is different from the home country. In SILC that's PB210 = OTH ("Country of birth - any other than country of residence")
  • Better definitions:
    Card: people whose citizenship status is either non-citizen or naturalized citizen
    Lelkes: that or people born in another country
    Also: Inside-EU and Outside-EU immigrants

Main question:

Where are there less inequalities between migrant and native population? Where are there less inequalities within migrant population?

How to measure inequality?

  • Indices (survey package)

    - Gini
    - Quantile Share Ratios
    - Atkinson
    - At Risk of Poverty

  • Oaxaca-Blinder Decomposition

    - For the general populace
    - Within migrant and native populations

Many problems

  • Questions

    - Do migrants participate in SILC less?
    - Can you really compare Germany and UK? (institutions, history, etc.)
    - If you can, how to interpret results?
    - Account for dropouts, better definition of migrants, finalize decompositions
